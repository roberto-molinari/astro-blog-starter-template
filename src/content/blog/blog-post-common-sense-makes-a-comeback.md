---
title: "Common Sense Makes a Comeback"
description: "Thoughts on common sense, as inspired by Joe Strummer"
pubDate: "May 26, 2026"
---

The late, great, Joe Strummer has [a
song](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VXdj9rrKTuE) with a line "common
sense makes a comeback" that has been running through my head recently.
With social media, AI, technology and so much data in more people's
lives, it feels like common sense is used less frequently now when
people think and make decisions. This is a problem because common sense
has a lot of utility both to individuals and society, when it\'s used
appropriately to guide thinking and make decisions.

Let's start by defining what we mean by "common sense" before getting
into why it\'s not used as much tody, and why it should be used more
often.

Asking google "what is common sense" gets this response (bolding is
mine):

> "Common sense is the basic level of **practical judgment, reasoning,
> and knowledge** **that people are expected to share** and use without
> needing specialized training. It applies to obvious, everyday
> situations---like checking both ways before crossing the street or
> realizing that a laptop turned off because the battery died" (citing
> [this Wikipedia page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_sense))

In a pre-internet society, where information had fewer/slower/less
accessible sources of knowledge (books, newspapers and people being the
primary sources), common sense was an important tool for basic
decision-making like the examples given in the quote. It also was a
useful tool for decision making beyond the basic day-to-day examples.
For example, before the internet, if someone was on a 2-hour drive, and
15 minutes into that drive their gas gauge was hitting on E, they would
stop and get gas at the first available gas station. It was just common
sense to do that, and the lack of other information/knowledge (like
other gas station locations that may be cheaper) wasn't at hand, so you
just made the common sense decision to get gas asap. The same holds true
for a lot of purchasing decisions which would come down to "if the
option at hand is good enough, just take it". Good enough is of course
relative to the person and situation, but you get the point.

But, as the quote also mentions, common sense gave people a way to align
expectations with other people because others were expected to share
similar common sense. People in a group (like a friends circle, family
or workplace) would rely on each other to apply common sense of their
own to get things done and make decisions. Because common sense can
sometimes be personalized (see the \"when do I stop to get gas" example
which is personalized v. the "look both ways before crossing the street"
example which, hopefully, is not), the pervasiveness of common sense
meant more trust in other individuals and less second guessing.

For the sake of conversation, let\'s say that pre-internet, people used
common sense to make decisions 50% of the time. And the 50% of the time
they did not, it was because common sense just wouldn't apply (i.e. they
truly needed deep knowledge/experience/insights that they could only get
from a slower source).

So, before the internet:

- Information and knowledge were slower/harder to access

- Forced people to use common sense for not just basic decisions (like
  to look both ways before crossing the street, but also purchasing
  decsiosns)

- So more common sense was used, and deeper analysis/critical thinking
  was "saved" for decisions that were both important and could wait

In a post-internet society, things have changed across all these areas.
With the internet, we have more / faster / more accessible sources of
information/knowledge (google, various websites/apps/sensors, social
media) often right in our hands always on our phones. That leads people
to reduce their reliance on common sense because there's a perception
that there's a better answer easily at hand. This reduces people\'s use
of common sense and erodes some of that shared context and approach
people had to getting things done and making decisions. Even worse, by
removing commonsense from some percentage of decision making, people may
need to do more critical thinking than they did before (if they don't
apply critical thinking, they'll blindly follow what the source of
knowledge tells them to do, which might be even worse). So, what could
have been a common-sense driven decision, turns into a more complex and
(ironically) time-consuming decision that taxes critical thinking
energy.

So, after the internet:

- Information became faster/easier to access data/information/knowledge

- Enabled people to leverage data/information/knowledge for more
  decisions

- So less common sense was used less, and deeper analysis/critical
  thinking is done (at least at times) for decisions regardless of
  importance or timeliness

The result is a greater reliance on technology (and the
data/information/knowledge it makes readily available) and less reliance
on common sense, while driving up the amount of critical thinking a
person does while driving down the shared sense of common knowledge that
we share as a society.
